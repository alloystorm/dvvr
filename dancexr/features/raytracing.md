# Raytracing Effects

Raytracing effects add physically accurate light transport to the
scene — reflections, shadows, ambient occlusion, and global
illumination calculated by tracing rays rather than relying on
screen-space approximations. The result is noticeably more realistic
lighting, especially with metallic surfaces, backlit characters, and
complex stage geometry. These effects are available on hardware that
supports DirectX Raytracing (DXR) or Vulkan Ray Tracing.

All raytracing features share a quality-versus-performance trade-off:
higher sample counts and bounce depths produce cleaner, more realistic
results but cost more GPU time. Start with lower settings and increase
them until the visual improvement is no longer worth the frame-rate hit.


## Reflection

Raytraced reflections replace screen-space reflections (SSR) with
accurate mirror-like and glossy reflections generated by tracing
rays from the camera through the scene. Unlike SSR, raytraced
reflections correctly handle objects that are off-screen, behind
the reflective surface, or hidden behind the camera.

**Roughness** controls how sharp or blurry the reflections appear —
low values give crisp mirror reflections (good for polished floors
and metal props), while high values produce soft, diffuse highlights
(cloth, skin). **Max Bounces** sets how many times a reflection ray
reroutes off successive surfaces. One bounce handles the first
reflection; two bounces lets you see the reflection of a reflection
(e.g. a character reflected in a mirror that also shows the wall
behind them). Each additional bounce roughly doubles the compute
cost, so keep this at 1 or 2 unless you have GPU headroom.

**Sample Count** determines how many rays are cast per pixel for
the reflection calculation. Low sample counts (4–8) converge quickly
but show visible noise or grain. Medium counts (16–32) produce
clean results suitable for most scenes. High counts (64+) are for
render-quality output where denoising can't fully clean the image.
**Denoise** applies a temporal filter that smooths out remaining
noise at a small sharpness cost — always leave this on for
real-time use.


## Ambient Occlusion

Raytraced ambient occlusion (AO) calculates how much ambient light
reaches each point on a surface by tracing rays in a hemisphere
around it. This produces much more accurate shadowing in crevices,
under clothing, and between close objects than screen-space AO
algorithms.

The **Radius** parameter controls how far the occlusion rays search
for blockers. Smaller radii capture fine detail (wrinkles, folds, gaps
between fingers) but miss broader shadowing. Larger radii capture
wider contact shadows but can over-darken open areas. A radius of
1–2 meters works well for full-body scenes; lower for close-ups.

**Bias** offsets the ray origin slightly to avoid self-shadowing
artefacts — raise it if you see dark banding on flat surfaces.
**Intensity** scales the darkness of the occlusion effect. Start
at 1.0 and adjust up for moody, high-contrast scenes or down for
bright, evenly lit environments. **Sample Count** and **Denoise**
work the same way as in the Reflection panel — more samples reduce
noise; denoising smooths residual grain.


## Global Illumination

Raytraced global illumination (GI) simulates light bouncing from
surface to surface — the primary light source illuminates a wall,
which in turn casts coloured light onto nearby objects. Without GI,
indirect lighting is baked or approximated; with it, light behaves
physically, producing realistic colour bleeding and bounce lighting.

**Bounces** controls how many times a GI ray scatters before being
absorbed. One bounce catches the first indirect light (sufficient for
most indoor scenes). Two bounces adds secondary scattering, useful for
brightly lit rooms with many reflective surfaces. Three or more
bounces are rarely needed and increase noise significantly.

**Strength** scales the brightness of the indirect light. A value of
1.0 is physically plausible; higher values exaggerate bounce lighting
for a stylised glow effect. Lower values reduce indirect contribution,
making direct light sources dominate the scene. **Sample Count** and
**Denoise** again mirror the other raytracing panels — start with
16–32 samples and enable denoising for real-time use.


## Shadow

Raytraced shadows replace shadow-map-based techniques with per-pixel
shadow rays that produce accurate, alias-free shadows at any
distance. Unlike shadow maps, raytraced shadows have no resolution
limit and correctly handle transparent and semi-transparent occluders.

**Softness** controls the penumbra width of shadows — low values
produce hard-edged shadows like direct sunlight, while high values
create the soft, diffuse shadows of an overcast day or an area light.
**Sample Count** trades noise for quality; 4–8 samples are enough
for acceptably soft shadows in real time, while still frames benefit
from 32+. Enable **Denoise** to clean up remaining grain with a
temporal filter.

For contact-hardening realism, pair raytraced shadows with the
[raytraced ambient occlusion](#ambient-occlusion) effect — AO fills
in the tight contact shadows that shadow rays alone might miss.


## Contact Shadow

Contact shadows are fine-scale occlusion shadows that appear where
two surfaces nearly touch — the dark line where a character's foot
meets the floor, or the shadow under a collar fold. This is
technically a specialised form of ambient occlusion that focuses
on very short ray distances.

**Intensity** controls how dark the contact shadow appears. **Radius**
is the search distance for nearby occluders — keep this small (0.1–0.5
meters) to limit the effect to actual contact points. Raising the
radius makes the effect bleed into wider areas, blurring the
distinction from normal ambient occlusion.

Contact shadows are cheap relative to full AO and should usually be
left on for realism, especially when characters are close to the
[ground](/dancexr/features/ground) or stage geometry. They add
noticeable grounding detail at minimal performance cost.


## Performance Tuning

Raytracing is GPU-intensive. Here is a practical order for reducing
cost without losing the most visible benefits:

1. Lower **Sample Count** on all effects (start at 4 for real-time).
2. Reduce **Bounces** on Reflection and GI to 1.
3. Turn off **Global Illumination** — it is the most expensive effect.
4. Switch Reflection to a lower resolution if available.

Conversely, for a showcase render, boost samples to 64+, enable
all effects, and disable denoising for maximum sharpness. The stage
presets in [Ground](/dancexr/features/ground) are a good test bed for
tuning — the pool and ocean presets in particular highlight
reflection and GI quality.
